Endocrine Control in Pregnancy

  • Pregnancy involves complex hormonal interactions to regulate foetal development, maternal adaptation, and parturition.
  • The main endocrine disorders affecting pregnancy include:
    • Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM)
    • Pre-existing type 1 diabetes mellitus
    • Thyroid disorders
    • Adrenal disorders
  • Maternal obesity is a growing concern due to its potential effects on:
    • Pregnancy outcomes (e.g. pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes).
    • Infant obesity predisposition via foetal programming.

Foetal Growth and Development

Key Hormonal Regulators of foetal Growth:

  • Thyroid hormones – Essential for CNS development
  • Insulin – Stimulates foetal growth more than it regulates glucose levels
  • GH–IGF Axis (Growth Hormone – Insulin-like Growth Factor) – Major driver of foetal growth
  • Cortisol – Matures foetal organs, particularly the lungs
  • Steroids (Oestrogens & Progesterone) – Support pregnancy maintenance

Foetal Insulin and Maternal Diabetes Impact

  • In a diabetic mother, excess maternal glucose crosses the placenta, stimulating foetal insulin overproduction
  • This can cause:
    • Macrosomia (excess foetal growth) → Risk of shoulder dystocia at birth
    • Neonatal hypoglycaemia after birth due to continued high insulin production without maternal glucose supply

Cortisol and Lung Maturation

  • Near-term cortisol surge stimulates surfactant synthesis in foetal lungs
  • Surfactant reduces surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse and allowing normal gas exchange at birth
  • Clinical Relevance:
    • Dexamethasone is given to mothers in preterm labour to accelerate lung maturation and prevent neonatal respiratory distress syndrome (NRDS)

hCG and Thyroid Function in Pregnancy

  • hCG has a structural similarity to TSH, leading to increased thyroid activity in the first trimester
  • This causes transient low TSH levels—important to remember when interpreting thyroid function tests during early pregnancy
  • The foetal adrenal cortex produces:
    • Cortisol (for stress response and organ maturation)
    • DHEA/DHEAS, which is converted into oestrogens (oestradiol, oestrone, oestriol), with oestriol detectable in maternal urine from the end of the first trimester

Oxytocin and Parturition (Labour)

Oxytocin Overview

  • Primary Functions:
    • Uterine contractions during labour
    • Milk ejection during breastfeeding
  • Circulatory Half-life: ~2–5 minutes
  • Regulation:
    • Minimal secretion outside pregnancy/lactation
    • Binds to GPCRs, activating intracellular calcium release, increasing uterine contractility

Hormonal Changes Leading to Birth

  • Parturition (Labour) Onset:
    • Trigger remains unclear
    • Progesterone levels fall → Removes inhibition of contractions
    • Oestrogen levels rise → Sensitises uterus to oxytocin and prostaglandins
    • Oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions, leading to foetal expulsion
  • Prostaglandins:
    • Locally produced in the uterus
    • Increase uterine contractility
    • Clinically used to induce labour by softening the cervix

The Ferguson Reflex (Positive Feedback Loop in Labour)

  • foetal descent stretches cervix → Triggers oxytocin release from the posterior pituitary
  • Oxytocin increases uterine contractions, further pushing the foetus down
  • More stretch → more oxytocin = Stronger contractions (positive feedback)
  • Feedback loop stops only after birth

Lactation and Breastfeeding

Breast Development

  • At birth: Mammary glands contain only lactiferous ducts, no alveoli
  • During puberty: Oestrogen induces alveolar growth
  • With menstrual cycling: Oestrogen + progesterone promote ductal-lobular growth, increasing breast size due to:
    • Fat deposition
    • Growth of connective tissue

Hormonal Regulation of Milk Secretion

  • Postpartum:
    • Progesterone drops, removing inhibition on prolactin
    • Prolactin remains high for weeks, sustaining milk production
    • Without suckling, milk secretion stops within 3–4 weeks

Milk-Ejection Reflex (Let-Down Reflex)

  • Triggered by:
    • Nipple stimulation (suckling)
    • Psychological conditioning (e.g. baby crying)
  • Pathway:
    • Sensory input → Hypothalamus → Posterior pituitary → Oxytocin release
    • Oxytocin stimulates contraction of alveolar myoepithelial cells, pushing milk toward the nipple

Prolactin and Milk Production

  • Suckling activates a neuroendocrine reflex, stimulating prolactin release from the anterior pituitary
  • More nipple stimulation = More prolactin = More milk production
  • Greater prolactin release when both breasts are stimulated (e.g. twins)

Lactation and Fertility

  • Lactation delays return of ovulation and menstruation
    • Normal menstrual cycles resume ~3–6 months postpartum
    • Some women ovulate before their first period postpartum, leading to unplanned pregnancies
  • Lactational Amenorrhoea:
    • Prolactin suppresses gonadotrophins (LH & FSH), delaying ovulation
    • Approx. 50% of breastfeeding women conceive within 9 months postpartum if not using contraception

Oxytocin and Parental Bonding

  • Oxytocin surges during labour establish maternal behaviour
  • Postpartum hormonal changes (falling progesterone, rising oestrogen, prolactin, and oxytocin) promote:
    • Maternal instincts (e.g. nesting)
    • Social bonding with offspring
  • Low oxytocin levels linked to postpartum depression (~18%)

Common Reproductive Disorders (Case Studies)

Disruptions to Reproductive Endocrine Control

  • Puberty timing issues:
    • Delayed puberty: Growth hormone deficiency (GHD), constitutional delay
    • Precocious puberty: Early onset of secondary sexual characteristics
  • Amenorrhoea: Absence of menstruation
  • Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS): Ovarian dysfunction, excess androgens
  • Prolactinoma: Prolactin-secreting pituitary tumour causing menstrual irregularities, infertility, and galactorrhoea
  • Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH):
    • Excess adrenal androgens → Ambiguous genitalia in females

The GnRH Test (For Diagnosing Pubertal Disorders)

  • Used to differentiate between true precocious puberty and premature thelarche
  • Premature thelarche (breast development in infants):
    • Self-limiting and usually does not indicate early puberty

Key Takeaways

Main Hormones Controlling Male & Female Reproduction

  • Oxytocin – Labour contractions, milk ejection, bonding
  • Prolactin – Milk production, inhibits ovulation
  • Oestrogen – Ductal-lobular breast growth, uterus preparation
  • Progesterone – Pregnancy maintenance, milk production inhibition
  • hCG – Maintains corpus luteum, stimulates thyroid
  • Insulin – Regulates foetal growth
  • Cortisol – Foetal lung maturation, stress response

Applying Knowledge to Reproductive Endocrine Control

  • Understanding hormonal changes in pregnancy, birth, and lactation
  • Recognising hormone imbalances leading to reproductive disorders
  • Clinical applications of hormone therapy (e.g. dexamethasone, prolactin control, contraception, fertility treatments)
