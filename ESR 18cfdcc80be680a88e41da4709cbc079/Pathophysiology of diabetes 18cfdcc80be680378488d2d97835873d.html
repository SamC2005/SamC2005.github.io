Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

Type 1 diabetes - autoimmune reaction against beta cells leads to infiltration of the pancreatic islet by T-cells, destroying the gland

Type 2 diabetes - pancreatic islet not usually affected

Lack of insulin in T1DM - glucose transport and utilisation reduced —> ↑glucose

Lack of insulin —> unopposed ↑glucagon

Effects:

  • Protein
    • ↑ Protein breakdown
  • Carbs
    • ↑ Glyocogenolysis
    • ↑ Gluconeogenesis
    • ↑ Hepatic glucose output
  • Fats
    • ↑ Lipolysis
    • ↑ Ketogenesis
  • Patient then becomes catabolic and dehydrated because of polyuria
    • This progresses to decompensation, diabetic ketoacidosis, and coma

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

T2DM is a complex metabolic disorder

Insulin resistance

Observe commonly that for a given glucose level, pancreas releases more insulin to control blood sugar

In the short term, the pancreas can deal with this requirement, and the Islets enlarge. However, eventually it can’t keep up which results in insulin deficiency.

Inflammation seems to be due to accumulation of fat around viscera. Innate immune system recognises that the fat is in the wrong place. Excessive cytokine release, which eventually ends up at the liver and causes insulin resistance.

Individual patients may have a mix of:

  • Genetic predisposition to obesity
  • Epigenetic predisposition
  • Differing abilities to compensate
    • B-cell proliferation
    • Adipose tissue inflammatory response
    • Tendency to become insulin resistant
  • Different environmental exposures
    • Diet, activity
    • Intercurrent illness
    • Exposure to drugs (e.g. steroids)
    • Access to healthcare

Long term effects of diabetes

Complications of diabetes

  • Increased incidence of CVD
  • Peripheral vascular disease
  • Renal disease
  • Retinal disease
  • Lens opacity (cataracts)
  • Neuropathy
    • Autonomic (bladder, stomach, blood vessels, erections)
    • Sensory
    • Motor
  • Skin infections
  • Osteoarthritis

Glycation pathway

AGEs (end product) used to measure average blood sugar using HbA1c, as haemoglobin is particularly susceptible to glycation with sugar

  • Excess glucose increases Hb glucation
  • We use HbA1c as an easily accessible measure of glycaemia over past 90 days (RBC lifespan)

When a cell is exposed to a high level of glucose, it starts making free oxygen radicals, increasing oxidative stress.

Oxidative stress refers to the fact that during metabolism many reactions involve oxidation which need free radicals (ROSs like O2-, H2O2).

Catalase breaks down H2O2 into H2O and O2.

If ROS not removed sufficiently, oxidative damage in DNA, proteins, and lipids caused.

Sorbitol pathway

When glucose levels are high, sorbitol and fructose accumulate in cells since they diffuse relatively slowly.

This causes osmotic effects which may damage cells such as lens and nerve cells.

How are the complications caused?

  • Retinopathy: glycation of basement membrane (BM), exudation, bleeding
  • Cataracts: accumulation of polyols (sorbitol) in lens
  • Neuropathy: glycation of BM —> blood supply to nerve blocked + leakage of protein
  • Skin infections: poor blood supply leads to poor healing, high glucose levels encourage pathogenic growth

Kidneys

Thickened basement membrane in blood vessels and increased permeability to blood proteins —> leads to nephropathy

Foot infections

High glucose levels make a rich medium for organisms infecting the skin

Contributed to by damage to blood vessels

Sensory neuropathy leads to foot numbness and damage

Cardiovascular disease

Risk of CVD greater in patients with diabetes

Up to 80% of people with diabetes will die from CVD

  • preventable deaths if action is taken

Diabetes accelerates atherosclerosis:

  • higher oxidative stress
  • causes higher cholesterol
  • more inflammation

Optimum blood sugar control

  • ‘Normal’ HbA1c is <6.5% (<48 mmol/mol)
    • this is difficult to achieve without causing hypos
    • associated with more mortality
  • Aim for 6.5-7.5% (48-59 mmol/mol)
  • Quality of life paramount
