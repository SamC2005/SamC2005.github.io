Case presentation and the GH axis

Methods of Diagnosis

1. Clinical observation

  • Patient history: rule out non-endocrine causes (e.g. respiratory/cardiac/renal)
  • Appearance: proportionate growth?
  • Anthropometric measurements: growth velocity/bone age/pubertal stages etc.

2. Measure serum GH: immunoassay

  • Need 0.5ml blood

How to Interpret?

    - 24 hr profile, taking bloods every 20 mins
    - Major peaks during sleep stages 3/4
    - inconvenient for lab
  1. Abnormalities
  1. Dynamic function tests for GH secretion

    Provocative and suppression test

  1. Provocative test

    - Inject insulin, sampling every 20 mins for 4 hours
    - Known as ITT (
    insulin tolerance test)
    - risk of severe hypoglycaemia in children
    - GH rise due to fall in glucose, not rise in insulin



  1. Suppression test

    - Drink glucose solution, sampling every 20 mins for 4 hours
    - Suppression due to rise in glucose
    - No suppression seen with GH excess conditions e.g. pituitary tumour
    - Late rise due to fall in glucose in response to a natural rise in endogenous insulin

  1. GH secretion naturally declines with age

    - during and after 3rd decade

  1. IGF-1 levels

    - also measured by immunoassay not as widely used as GH measurements
    - confined to specialist centres

  1. IGF binding therapies
    - measured in specialist centres for difficult cases
    - research rather than routine

Major Clinical Conditions Associated with Growth Disorders

Growth Hormone Deficiency (GHD)

  • Can be congenital or acquired.
  • Features: Short stature, increased fat mass, delayed bone age, poor growth velocity.
  • Causes: Pituitary disorders, genetic mutations, cranial irradiation.

Laron Syndrome (GH Insensitivity)

  • Mutation in GH receptor leads to lack of IGF-1 production.
  • Features: Severe short stature, small facial features, obesity, insulin hypersensitivity.

Gigantism & Acromegaly (Excess GH)

  • Gigantism: GH excess before epiphyseal closure → excessive linear growth.
  • Acromegaly: GH excess in adulthood → bone and soft tissue overgrowth, metabolic issues.

Turner Syndrome (45, X0 Chromosome Abnormality)

  • Features: Short stature, gonadal dysgenesis, webbed neck, cardiovascular issues.
  • GH therapy is often used to improve height.

Precocious and Delayed Puberty

  • Precocious puberty: Early fusion of growth plates → short adult stature.
  • Delayed puberty: Prolonged growth potential but risk of psychosocial issues.

Skeletal Dysplasias (e.g., Achondroplasia)

  • Genetic defects affecting bone growth.
  • Features: Short stature, disproportionate limb length, normal trunk size.

Normal Growth Regulation & Features of Growth Disorders

  • GH Secretion:
    • Secreted by anterior pituitary somatotrophs.
    • Regulated by GHRH (stimulates) and somatostatin (inhibits).
    • Stimulated by sleep, exercise, fasting, arginine.
    • Inhibited by glucose, cortisol, free fatty acids.
  • GH Actions:
    • Direct: Stimulates metabolism (lipolysis, protein synthesis, insulin resistance).
    • Indirect: IGF-1 production from liver → promotes bone and soft tissue growth.
    • IGF-1 works through chondrocyte clonal expansion at growth plates.
  • GH Insensitivity (Laron Syndrome):
    • GH receptor mutation → failure to produce IGF-1 → severe short stature.
  • GH Excess (Gigantism & Acromegaly):
    • GH overproduction → excessive IGF-1 → increased bone and tissue growth.
  • GH & Metabolism:
    • GH opposes insulin, increasing blood glucose levels.
    • Induces lipolysis and protein anabolism.

Social Consequences of Short Stature

  • Psychosocial impact:
    • Increased risk of bullying, lower self-esteem, social withdrawal.
    • Perceived as younger than actual age → affects peer relationships.
    • Can impact academic and career opportunities.
  • Treatment Considerations:
    • GH therapy is beneficial but may not always be effective.
    • Ethical concerns regarding GH use for idiopathic short stature.

Anthropometric Measurements for Growth Assessment

  1. Height vs Age
    • <2 years: Supine length measurement.
    • >2 years: Standing height using a stadiometer.
  1. Growth Velocity (cm/year vs Age)
    • High in infancy, slows, then rapid pubertal growth spurt.
    • Falls to zero when puberty ends (sex steroids close growth plates).
  1. Bone Age (Skeletal Maturation)
    • X-ray of wrist compared to age-related reference standards.
    • Assesses growth potential (delayed in GH deficiency, advanced in precocious puberty).
  1. Pubertal Stages (Tanner Staging)
    • Tracks sexual maturation (breast, genital, pubic hair development).
    • Delayed puberty → growth potential prolonged.
  1. Height vs Chronological Age (Population Growth Charts)
    • Compares individual to reference population.
    • Helps identify abnormal growth patterns.
  1. Standard Deviation Score (SDS)
    • SDS = (Observed height – Mean height) / SD for that age & sex.
    • Normal range: -2 to +2 SDS.
