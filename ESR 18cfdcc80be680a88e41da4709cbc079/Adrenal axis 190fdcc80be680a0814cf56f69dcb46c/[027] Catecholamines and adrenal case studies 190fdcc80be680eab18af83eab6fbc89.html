Development of the Adrenal Gland

Embryonic Origins

  • The mesothelium at the anterior of the mesonephros forms the embryonic adrenal cortex.
  • Week 5: The embryonic cortex invades the retroperitoneal mesenchyme.
  • Week 7: Phaeochromaffinoblasts (neural crest-derived) invade the cortex and form the medulla.

Adrenal Cortex Structure from 3 years of age

  • Zona glomerulosa (mineralocorticoids)
  • Zona fasciculata (glucocorticoids)
  • Zona reticularis (androgens)

Adrenal Medulla

  • Composed of chromaffin cells, which secrete catecholamines.
  • It functions as a specialised sympathetic ganglion (no axons, but direct hormone release).

Vasculature

  • Blood supply to adrenal medulla via superior, middle and inferior arteries
  • Divide to form medullary cortical and capsular arterioles
  • Additional blood supply from cortical capillaries

Synthesis of Catecholamines

Tyrosine is the precursor

Enzymatic Pathway:

  1. Tyrosine → DOPA (via tyrosine hydroxylase, rate-limiting step, cytoplasmic)
  1. DOPA → Dopamine (via DOPA decarboxylase, cytoplasmic)
  1. Dopamine → Noradrenaline (via dopamine β-hydroxylase, occurs in granules)
  1. Noradrenaline → Adrenaline (via PNMT, cytoplasmic; stimulated by high glucocorticoid levels from cortex)

Control of Catecholamine Synthesis

PNMT Expression and Activity

PNMT expression & activity is increased by:

  • Sympathetic neural input
  • Glucocorticoids (high local concentration from cortex drainage)

Regulation of Synthesis

Acute Regulation:

  • Sympathetic stimulation activates tyrosine hydroxylase (rate-limiting step).

Chronic Regulation:

  • Sustained stimulation leads to increased tyrosine hydroxylase expression, increasing catecholamine output.

Regulation of Secretion

Storage & Secretion:

  • Stored in granules within chromaffin cells.
  • Secretion is stimulated by acetylcholine from preganglionic sympathetic fibres.

Cellular Actions of Catecholamines

  • Adrenoceptors:
    • α-adrenoceptors (generally excitatory, via PI-PLC/Ca²⁺ pathway).
    • β-adrenoceptors (generally inhibitory, via AC-cAMP-PKA pathway).
  • Effects of Activation:
    • Mobilises fuel stores (↑ glucose, ↑ free fatty acids).
    • Stimulates the heart (↑ rate & force).
    • Alters smooth muscle tone in respiratory, gastrointestinal, and urinary tracts.
    • Acts in the "fight or flight" response.

Adrenaline:

  • β1: ↑ heart rate & contractility.
  • β2: Bronchodilation, vasodilation (skeletal muscle).
  • α1: Vasoconstriction (skin, gut).

Noradrenaline:

  • α1: Vasoconstriction → ↑ blood pressure.
  • β1: Some increase in heart rate and contractility.
  • Less effect on bronchodilation (compared to adrenaline).

Metabolism of Catecholamines

  • Major Enzymes:
    • Monoamine oxidase (MAO)
    • Catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT)
  • Major Metabolites:
    • Metanephrine
    • Vanillylmandelic acid (VMA)

Control of Catecholamine Levels:

  • Uptake more important than metabolism for regulation
  • Post-ganglionic endocytosis is key for catecholamine clearance

Adrenomedullary Disorders

Hypofunction (Rare)

Causes: Failure to synthesise/secrete catecholamines.

Consequences: Hypotension & hypoglycaemia.

Management: No need to replace adrenaline post-adrenalectomy (corticosteroids are sufficient).

Hyperfunction

Hypersecretion of adrenaline results in

  • hypertension and hyperglycaemia
    • + insulin resistance

Phaeochromocytoma (Catecholamine-secreting Tumour):

Secretes both adrenaline & noradrenaline.

↳ plus dopamine, vanylmandelic acid, metanephrines

Symptoms

Hypertension, tachycardia, sweating, palpitations, headache.

Can be part of Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia (MEN) syndromes.

Diagnosis

Diagnosed via 24-hour urine catecholamines/metabolites.

Imaging: mIBG scans, MRI, PET.

Treatment

Surgical removal (risk of hypertensive crisis)
