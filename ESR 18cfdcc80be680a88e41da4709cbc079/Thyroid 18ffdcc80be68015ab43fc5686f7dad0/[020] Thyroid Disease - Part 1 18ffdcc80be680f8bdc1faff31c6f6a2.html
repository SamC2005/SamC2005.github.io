[020] Thyroid Disease - Part 1

  • describe the presentation of hypothyroidism, hyperthyroidism and thyroid goitre
  • understand the causes of these conditions
  • outline the diagnostic procedures
  • discuss treatment options

Hyperthyroidism

Symptoms

  • Also:
    • Tremor
    • Fatigue
    • Angina
    • Muscle weakness
    • Diarrhoea
    • Oligomenorrhoea and infertility
    • Eyelid retraction + lid lag

Specific to Graves’ disease:

  • Thyroid eye disease (TED)
    • Periorbital oedema
    • Proptosis
    • Diplopia
    • Corneal ulceration
    • Loss of visual acuity
  • Pretibial myxoedema
  • Thyroid acropachy

Causes of hyperthyroidism

  • Graves’ disease
  • Multimodal goitre or Toxic adenoma
  • Thyroiditis: subacute, Hashimoto
  • Ectopic TSH: hCG in early pregnancy, recombinant TSH
  • Drugs: amiodarone, exogenous iodine, thyroxine
  • Ectopic thyroid tissue: metastases from thyroid carcinoma, struma ovarii
  • Pituitary disease: TSHoma

Graves’ disease

  • Cause of ~50% of thyrotoxicosis
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Autoantibody against the TSH receptor
    • binds and activates the receptor
    • only example of an antibody which mimics a hormone

TSH-R Abs don't always stimulate

  • TSI = thyroid stimulating antibodies
  • TBII = TSH-R binding inhibitory immunoglobulins

Patients can produce both of the above, in different proportions during history of disease

Immunology of Graves’ disease

  • Genetic predisposition
    • HLA DR17, DQ2
  • Environmental trigger ( - virus?)
    • T cell infiltration
    • B and plasma cells
    • Cytokine production
    • Anti TSH-R Abs produced

Diagnostic tests

Thyroid autoantibodies

  • Anti thyroid peroxidase (TPO) and anti thyroglobulin (TG) are non-specific and insensitive
  • Anti TSH-R Abs: specific for Graves’ disease (95%)

Thyroid scintigram

  • Inject 99mTc-labelled pertechnate
  • Scan under gamma camera

Treatment

Medical Rx

  • Antithyroid drugs (OTU, carbimazole)
    • Useful for graves’ disease (50% remit at 18 months)
    • Toxic MNG/adenoma will not remit
    • Rash/uticaria/athralgia (>10%)
    • Agranulocytosis (0.2%)
  • Lugol’s iodine
    • Blocks production of T4/T3 by Wolff-Chaikoff effect
    • Effective for 10 days
    • Then becomes hyperthyroid (Jod-Basedow effect)

Radioiodine (131I)

  • Specificity of I- uptake by thyroid tissue
  • high energy β-particles (plus γ-radiation
  • Effective for toxic MNG/adenoma
  • Also used for Graves’ disease
  • Side effects:
    • hypothyroidism eventually in 70-80%
    • worsening of thyroid eye disease
  • Pregnancy
    • Not to get pregnant for 6/12 months after

Surgery

  • Unable to take drugs or RAI
  • Large goitre
  • Complications:
    • Hypothyrodism
    • Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury (1%)
    • Hypoparathyroidism (2% permanent)
    • Haemorrhage
    • Infection

Hypothyroidism

Symptoms

Causes

  • Autoimmune primary hypothyroidism
    • 2% population
    • 10x more common in women
  • Iatrogenic: surgery, RAI/anti-thyroid drugs
  • Thyroiditis - e.g. Hashimoto’s
  • Iodine deficiency - mountainous areas
  • Congenital
  • Secondary hypothyroidism: pituitary disease or surgery

Congenital hypothyroidism

  • 1:4000 births
  • Cretinism - severe neurological impairment
  • Iodine deficiency (commonest cause worldwide)
  • Dyshormogenesis (defect in thyroid synthesis)
  • Non-goitrous congenital hypothyroidism

So, NHS newborn screen for TSH (heel prick)

Treat with L-T4 as soon as possible

Iodine insufficiency

  • Supply of iodine in water depends upon local geology
    • Require a min. of 50μg of iodine/day
  • Problem typically in mountainous central land masses
    • European alps
    • Southern Alps in New Zealand
    • Himalayas
    • Africa (Ethiopia, Sudan, Algeria, Angola, Mozambique, Ghana, Morocco)

Dietary sources

  • Salt (not in UK)
  • Seafood and seaweed
  • Dairy products
    • from iodine used in tank cleaning
  • Iodised oil
    • for mass public health treatment

Thyroid can compensate:

  • Increased iodine uptake
  • increased iodine recycling
  • MIT preferentially formed over DIT
    • So the T3:T4 ratio increases
  • This will maintain euthyroid status for a while
  • With time a goitre forms as ↑TSH

Dishormonogenesis

  • Genetic defects in:
    • NIS, Pendrin
    • Thyroglobulin
    • Thyroperoxidase, iodothyrosine deiodinase, dual oxidase 2

Non-goitrous congenital hypothyroidism

  • Defect in embryological development
    • PAX8
    • NKX2-5
  • Defect in TSH-R or TSH itself
    • TSH-R loss of function
    • TSHB
  • Distinguished from dishormonogenesis due to no goitre

Secondary hypothyroidism

  • Pituitary/hypothalamic disease (e.g. tumour, surgery, head injury)
  • TSH low or normal in these cases
  • Can be ‘inappropriately normal’ given low T4, T3

General Treatment

  • Levothyroxine (T4)
  • Liothyronine (T3)
  • Thyroid extract
    • cow, pigs, T3>T4 so not suitable
  • Treat and titrate to normal TSH
