Defects in the Control of Adrenal Steroidogenesis

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis regulates adrenal steroid output.

Increased ACTH drive

Increased ACTH drive occurs due to:

  • Chronic stress (↑ CRH)
  • Pituitary tumours secreting ACTH
  • Glucocorticoid resistance (loss of negative feedback)
  • Ectopic ACTH secretion (e.g., small cell lung carcinoma)
  • Defects in cortisol synthesis (e.g., Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH), Addison’s disease)

Cushing’s disease and syndrome

Cushing’s disease

Pituitary tumour leading to excess ACTH

Cushing’s syndrome

Any cause of excess cortisol

  • e.g. chronic steroid use, adrenal tumour, ectopic ACTH

Diagnosis

  • Late-night salivary cortisol
    • Elevated cortisol between 23:00-00:00 is the earliest detectable abnormality
  • Urine free cortisol
    • 24 hour free cortisol level reflects cortisol secretion throughout the day.
      • However, ~20-25% of Cushing’s syndrome pts have normal urine cortisol
  • Low-dose dexamethasone suppression testing

Differential diagnostic testing

  • ACTH results

  • CT of adrenal glands
  • MRI of pituitary gland
    • To distinguish pituitary from non-pituitary ACTH-secreting tumours
      • However, ~10% of the population have incidental pituitary tumours
  • Inferior petrosal sinus sampling
    • For patients with ACTH-secreting tumour causing Cushing’s syndrome and a normal pituitary MRI.
      • Requires a skilled invasive radiologist who can sample blood from the veins that drain the pituitary. Can test for ACTh before and after administration of CRH

Treatment

  • Surgery directed at the adrenal or pituitary is first line treatment
    • however many patients don’t have a sustained remission
  • Drugs:
    • Targets cortisol synthesis and release at the adrenal level, lowering it
      • Ketoconazole (anti-fungal)
      • Mitotane (adrenal cancer med)
      • Metyrapone
    • Or drug blocks action of cortisol
      • Mifepristone
    • In patients with pituitary disease
      • Cabergoline
      • Pasireotide

Addison’s disease

Causes

  • Primary adrenal insufficiency (↓ cortisol & aldosterone).
  • Autoimmune adrenalitis (70-90% of cases in the UK).
  • Tuberculosis (worldwide leading cause).
  • Iatrogenic (sudden withdrawal of long-term steroids).

Symptoms

Early symptoms

  • Fatigue, weakness, low mood.
  • Weight loss, anorexia.
  • Salt cravings 

    (↓aldosterone → Na+ loss).

Late symptoms

  • Hyperpigmentation (↑ ACTH → ↑ MSH).
  • Postural hypotension (↓ aldosterone).
  • Hypoglycaemia (↓ cortisol).
  • Electrolyte imbalance (↓ Na+, ↑ K+).

Adrenal Crisis (medical emergency):

  • Severe hypotension, hypoglycaemia, dehydration.
  • Management: IV hydrocortisone + fluids

Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia

  • Autosomal recessive enzyme deficiencies → defective cortisol synthesis.
  • Most common cause: 21-hydroxylase deficiency (90% of CAH cases).
  • Results in:
    • ↓ Cortisol & aldosterone → ↑ ACTH drive → adrenal hyperplasia.
    • ↑ Androgens → virilisation in females.

Types of CAH

Salt-Wasting CAH (21-hydroxylase deficiency [P450C21_{C21}C21​﻿], severe form)

  • ↓ Aldosterone & cortisol → hypotension, Na+ loss, hyperkalaemia.
  • ↑ Androgens → ambiguous genitalia in females.
  • Life-threatening adrenal crisis in neonates.

Simple-Virilising (Salt-Sparing) CAH

  • Milder → Enough aldosterone to prevent salt loss.
  • Features: Virilisation without hypotension.
  • 11β-Hydroxylase [P450C11B1_{C11B1}C11B1​﻿] deficiency
    • ↓ Cortisol & aldosterone.
    • ↑ DOC (deoxycorticosterone) → hypertension.
    • ↑ Androgens → virilisation in females.

Iatrogenic Cushing’s & Steroid Withdrawal

  • Chronic corticosteroid use suppresses CRH & ACTH → adrenal atrophy
    • Risk pf iatrogenic Addison’s syndrome
  • Features of iatrogenic Cushing’s:
    • Moon face, buffalo hump, central obesity.
    • Osteoporosis, diabetes, skin thinning.
  • Steroid withdrawal must be gradual to prevent adrenal crisis.

  • Cushing’s disease vs syndrome:
    • Disease: Pituitary tumour leading to excess ACTH.
    • Syndrome: Any cause of excess cortisol (chronic steroid use, adrenal tumour, ectopic ACTH).

Molecular Defects in the Synthesis of Corticosteroids and Neonatal Consequences

  • Corticosteroid synthesis occurs in the adrenal cortex.
  • Defects in enzymes cause Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH):
    • 21-hydroxylase deficiency (most common, 90% of cases)
    • 11β-hydroxylase deficiency (excess deoxycorticosterone (DOC) → hypertension)
    • 17α-hydroxylase deficiency (↓ androgens → ambiguous genitalia in males)
  • Neonatal consequences:
    • Cortisol deficiency → impaired stress response
    • Aldosterone deficiency → salt-wasting crisis
    • Androgen excess (in 21-hydroxylase deficiency) → virilisation of female neonates

Molecular Causes and Symptoms of Salt-Wasting and Salt-Sparing CAH

  • Salt-wasting CAH:
    • Severe 21-hydroxylase deficiency → ↓ aldosterone & cortisol → Na+ loss, dehydration, hypotension.
  • Salt-sparing CAH:
    • Milder 21-hydroxylase deficiency → some aldosterone production maintained → virilisation without salt loss.
  • Malignant hypertension in 11β-hydroxylase CAH:
    • DOC accumulation acts as a mineralocorticoid, increasing Na+ retention & BP.

Iatrogenic Cushing’s Syndrome and Addison’s Disease

  • Iatrogenic Cushing’s:
    • Excess exogenous glucocorticoids → suppression of CRH & ACTH → adrenal atrophy.
    • Features: moon face, truncal obesity, easy bruising, striae, osteoporosis.
    • Patients must be weaned off steroids to prevent adrenal insufficiency.
  • Addison’s disease:
    • Autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex (most common cause in the UK).
    • Symptoms: fatigue, weight loss, hyperpigmentation (↑ ACTH stimulates melanocytes), hypotension, hypoglycaemia.
    • Adrenal crisis (life-threatening): Requires urgent IV hydrocortisone & fluids.

Cellular Basis of Symptoms of Increased/Decreased Glucocorticoids & Mineralocorticoids

  • Excess glucocorticoids (e.g., Cushing’s syndrome):
    • Muscle wasting (protein breakdown).
    • Hyperglycaemia (gluconeogenesis).
    • Central obesity (lipid metabolism disruption).
  • Deficient glucocorticoids (e.g., Addison’s disease):
    • Hypoglycaemia (impaired gluconeogenesis).
    • Fatigue, weight loss.
  • Excess mineralocorticoids (e.g., hyperaldosteronism):
    • Hypertension, hypokalaemia, metabolic alkalosis.
  • Deficient mineralocorticoids (e.g., salt-wasting CAH, Addison’s disease):
    • Hypotension, hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis.

Cellular Causes of Resistance to Glucocorticoids and Mineralocorticoids

  • Glucocorticoid resistance:
    • Mutation in the glucocorticoid receptor (GR) → loss of negative feedback → ↑ ACTH → adrenal hyperplasia.
    • Symptoms: Cushingoid features but normal cortisol levels.
  • Mineralocorticoid resistance:
    • Pseudohypoaldosteronism: Target cells do not respond to aldosterone → Na+ loss, hyperkalaemia, hypotension.
