Overview of Reproductive Hormones

The main hormones responsible for reproductive control include:

  • Gonadotropin-Releasing Hormone (GnRH) – Released from the hypothalamus in a pulsatile manner; stimulates the anterior pituitary
  • Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH) – Secreted by the anterior pituitary; regulates gametogenesis (spermatogenesis in males, follicular development in females)
  • Luteinising Hormone (LH) – Secreted by the anterior pituitary; stimulates sex steroid production (testosterone in males, oestrogen/progesterone in females)
  • Testosterone (T) – Produced by Leydig cells; essential for male secondary sexual characteristics and spermatogenesis
  • Oestrogen (E2) – Secreted by developing follicles; regulates female reproductive cycles and secondary sexual characteristics
  • Progesterone (P4) – Secreted by the corpus luteum; maintains the endometrium for potential pregnancy
  • Inhibin – Secreted by Sertoli cells (males) and granulosa cells (females); negatively regulates FSH secretion

Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) Axis

Hormonal feedback

Hypothalamus: Releases GnRH in a pulsatile fashion

Anterior Pituitary: Responds to GnRH by secreting FSH and LH

Gonads:

  • Males: LH stimulates Leydig cells to produce testosterone, which supports spermatogenesis in Sertoli cells(FSH also supports Sertoli function)
  • Females: LH and FSH regulate follicular development and steroidogenesis

Feedback Mechanisms

  • Negative Feedback:

    Testosterone (in males), oestrogen/progesterone (in females), and inhibin all inhibit GnRH, FSH, and LH secretion

  • Positive Feedback:

    During late follicular phase in females, high oestrogen levels trigger an LH surge, leading to ovulation

Female Reproductive Cycle

Female Sexual Reproduction

Phases of the Menstrual Cycle (28 days)

Follicular Phase (Days 1–14)

  • FSH stimulates follicular growth; dominant follicle secretes oestrogen
  • Rising oestrogen levels initially exert negative feedback on FSH/LH
  • Near ovulation, oestrogen exerts positive feedback, triggering an LH surge

Ovulation (Day 14)

  • LH surge causes rupture of the dominant follicle, releasing the ovum

Luteal Phase (Days 15–28)

  • The ruptured follicle forms the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone (and some oestrogen)
  • Progesterone maintains the endometrial lining
  • If no fertilisation occurs, corpus luteum degenerates, leading to decreased progesterone/oestrogen and menstruation

Menstruation (Days 1–5)

  • Shedding of the endometrial lining due to falling hormone levels

Hormonal Graph of the Menstrual Cycle

Oestrogen rises during follicular phase and peaks before ovulation.

LH and FSH peak at ovulation.

Progesterone dominates in the luteal phase.

If pregnancy occurs, the corpus luteum is maintained by hCG (human chorionic gonadotropin).

Summary

Hormonal Regulation of Spermatogenesis

Spermatogenesis

(Occurs in the Seminiferous Tubules)

  1. FSH stimulates Sertoli cells to support sperm maturation
  1. LH stimulates Leydig cells to produce testosterone
  1. Testosterone is essential for sperm production and is maintained at high levels in the testes via androgen-binding protein (ABP)

Negative Feedback Regulation

  • Testosterone and inhibin suppress FSH and LH secretion to regulate sperm production

Puberty and Gonadal Maturation

  • Gonadarche: Maturation of gonads; begins at puberty due to increased GnRH pulsatility
  • Adrenarche: Maturation of adrenal glands, leading to increased androgens (DHEA, DHEAS)
  • Menarche: Onset of first menstruation (requires ~17-22% body fat)

Key Factors Affecting Puberty

  • Body weight: Critical body fat percentage (~22%) is needed for reproductive function
  • Leptin: Plays a permissive role in puberty but does not directly trigger it
  • Environmental factors: Increased BMI and better nutrition have led to an earlier onset of puberty

Clinical Considerations

Precocious Puberty (Early Onset)

  • Occurs before age 8 in girls, 9 in boys
  • Causes: CNS lesions, hormone imbalances (e.g., McCune-Albright syndrome)

Delayed Puberty

  • No breast development by 13 (or menstruation by 15) or testicular growth by 14
  • Causes: Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (e.g. Kallmann Syndrome - ↓GnRH), chromosomal abnormalities (Klinefelter's Syndrome (XXY), Turner Syndrome)
