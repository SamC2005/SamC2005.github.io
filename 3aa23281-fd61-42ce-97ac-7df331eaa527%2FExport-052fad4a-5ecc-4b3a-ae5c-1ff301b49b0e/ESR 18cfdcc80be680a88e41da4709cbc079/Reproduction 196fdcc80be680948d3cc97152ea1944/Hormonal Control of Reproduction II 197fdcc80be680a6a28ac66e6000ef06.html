Hormonal Control of Reproduction II

  • Describe the changes that occur as cell change from primordial germ cells into gametes describe how the morphology of gametes relates to the established roles in fertilization.
  • Describe the timeline of development of the zygote until implantation.
  • Establish the changes that need occur to maintain pregnancy

Development of Sexual Phenotype

  • At Fertilisation:
    • The sperm (carrying either an X or Y chromosome) fuses with an X-bearing ovum
    • This event establishes the genetic sex
  • Around 4 Weeks Gestation:
    • Proliferation of cells in the urogenital ridge creates a bipotential (undifferentiated) gonad
  • Around 7 Weeks Gestation (Sex Determination):
    • In a 46,XY embryo, the gonad differentiates into a testis
    • In a 46,XX embryo, the gonad remains as an ovary

Human Life Cycle, Gamete Numbers, and Female Fecundity

  • Human Life Cycle Overview:
    • At birth, individuals are physically and sexually immature
    • Sexual maturity is achieved during adolescence
  • Differences in Fecundity:
    • Males: Once mature, sperm production is maintained throughout most of life
    • Females: Fecundity is time-limited. Egg quality declines markedly after about 35 years, ending at menopause (~50 years)
  • Gamete Numbers and Egg Wastage:
    • Unlike external fertilisers (e.g. frogs), mammals fertilise internally; thus, only one or two eggs are released per cycle
    • A female foetus peaks with around 7 million oocytes at 6 months of foetal life
    • Most oocytes are lost (atresia) during foetal, neonatal, and pubertal life, leaving only a few hundred available during the reproductive years

Spermatogenesis

  • Process Overview:
    • Primordial germ cells arrest as spermatogonia until puberty
    • Spermatogenesis Stages:
      • A basal spermatogonial stem cell divides mitotically to renew itself and produce a primary spermatocyte
      • The primary spermatocyte enters the adluminal compartment and undergoes the first meiotic division to form haploid secondary spermatocytes
      • Secondary spermatocytes quickly undergo the second meiotic division, producing spermatids that mature into spermatozoa
    • Mature spermatozoa are released into the lumen and travel to the epididymis for storage until ejaculation

Gamete Quality and Fertilisation Requirements

  • Gamete Quality:
    • ~ 40% of sub-fertility cases are due to male factors (e.g. poor sperm quality)
    • ~ 20% are related to poor egg quality
    • ~ 30% of miscarriages are linked to chromosomal errors in the egg
  • Fertilisation Requires High-Quality Gametes:
    • The integrity and functionality of both sperm and egg are essential for successful fertilisation

Fertilisation Process

Window of Opportunity

  • Timing for Fertilisation:
    • Sperm are viable for about 48 hours after deposition
    • The secondary oocyte remains viable for approximately 24 hours post-ovulation
    • Fertilisation is most likely to occur during a ~3-day window (roughly 2 days before to 1 day after ovulation)

Sperm Changes and Capacitation

  • Sperm Movement:
    • Sperm use flagellar movement to swim from the vagina into the cervical canal
    • Contractions of the uterus and oviduct assist in propelling sperm further
    • Prostaglandins in Semen:
      • These compounds stimulate uterine contractions, aiding sperm in reaching the oviduct
  • Capacitation:
    • Spermatozoa require time in the female reproductive tract to achieve full fertilising capacity
    • This involves:
      • A change to a hyperactivated motility state
      • Initiation of the acrosome reaction

Sperm Penetration and Acrosome Reaction

Layers to Penetrate:

  • Corona Radiata: The layer of granulosa cells surrounding the secondary oocyte
  • Zona Pellucida: A glycoprotein layer that contains the sperm receptor ZP3

Acrosome Reaction:

  • The acrosome, located over the sperm head, releases enzymes (e.g. hyaluronidase and acrosin) that digest the zona pellucida
  • Binding of ZP3 to receptors on the sperm triggers this reaction
  • Although many sperm undergo the acrosome reaction, only the first sperm to penetrate the zona and reach the oocyte’s plasma membrane can fuse, initiating fertilisation
  • Polyspermy Prevention:
    • Fusion triggers a rapid depolarisation of the oocyte membrane, preventing additional sperm from entering

Pronuclei Formation and Zygote Creation

  • Post-Fusion Events:
    • Once a sperm fuses with the oocyte, the oocyte completes meiosis II, dividing into a larger ovum and a small second polar body
    • The sperm nucleus forms the male pronucleus, while the oocyte nucleus forms the female pronucleus
    • Fusion of these pronuclei restores the diploid number (46 chromosomes), creating the zygote
  • Fertilisation as a Protracted Process:
    • Although often simplified as a single event, fertilisation involves multiple hours of sequential steps from sperm transport to pronuclear fusion

Establishment of Pregnancy

Conceptus Communication and Pre-Implantation Development

  • Conceptus Signalling:
    • After fertilisation, the two-cell conceptus must signal its presence to the mother to transition her reproductive system from a cyclic to a pregnant state
    • This signalling is critical to prevent the normal regression (luteolysis) of the corpus luteum
    • Failure in signalling would lead to a fall in progesterone and the loss of the conceptus
  • Pre-Implantation Period:
    • The conceptus remains in the oviduct for several days before transfer into the uterus
    • This transfer is facilitated by the rising ratio of progesterone to oestrogen during the early luteal phase

Implantation Process

  • Attachment Phases:
    • Close Apposition: The blastocyst aligns closely with the endometrial surface
    • Adherence: The endometrium and blastocyst interact more intimately, with proteolytic enzymes (from trophoblast cells or uterine secretions) removing the zona pellucida
  • Invasive Implantation:
    • In humans, the blastocyst rapidly invades the endometrial tissue
    • The trophoblast erodes the surface epithelium, and the conceptus embeds within the maternal stroma
    • The depth of invasion may vary, and in some conditions, the maternal epithelium can even reform over the implanted conceptus (interstitial implantation)

Hormonal Maintenance of Pregnancy

Maternal Recognition of Pregnancy and hCG Role

  • Conceptus Signalling:
    • Once implantation begins, the conceptus must signal its presence to prevent the normal luteolysis that would occur in a non-pregnant cycle
    • This is achieved by the secretion of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) by the developing trophoblast
  • hCG Details:
    • hCG is similar to luteinising hormone (LH) and binds to LH receptors, thereby maintaining the corpus luteum and its progesterone production
    • High levels of hCG are associated with symptoms such as severe early morning vomiting (hyperemesis gravidarum)
    • hCG is also the hormone detected by most pregnancy tests (urine assays), often as early as 3 weeks after fertilisation

Corpus Luteum Function and Transition to Placental Support

  • In the Non-Pregnant State:
    • The corpus luteum lasts approximately 14 days before regressing into the corpus albicans due to lack of luteotrophic support
  • In Early Pregnancy:
    • Within about 2 weeks of fertilisation, hCG from the conceptus maintains the corpus luteum, ensuring continued production of progesterone
    • After another 2–3 weeks, the conceptus begins synthesising its own steroidal hormones
  • Feto-Placental Unit:
    • By the end of the first trimester, the placenta and foetus together form a feto-placental unit that takes over the production of progesterone
    • This transition reduces the reliance on the corpus luteum, which becomes less significant as the pregnancy progresses
