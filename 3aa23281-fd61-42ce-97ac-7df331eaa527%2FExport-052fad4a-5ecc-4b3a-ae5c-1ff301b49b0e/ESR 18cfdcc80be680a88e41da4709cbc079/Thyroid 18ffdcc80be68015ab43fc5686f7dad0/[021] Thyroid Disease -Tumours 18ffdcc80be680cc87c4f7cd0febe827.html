[021] Thyroid Disease -Tumours

Thyroid Nodules

Clinical history

  • Rapid growth, dysphagia, stridor, or lymphadenopathy → suspicious features.
  • Previous radiation exposure (e.g., Chernobyl, Fukushima).
  • Age: Higher risk in very young (<16) or older patients (>60).

Physical examination:

  • Palpation for size, consistency, retrosternal extension.

Investigations:

  • Thyroid function tests (TFTs): Hyperthyroidism reduces but does not rule out malignancy.
  • Ultrasound (US):
    • High sensitivity for detecting nodules but cannot distinguish benign vs malignant.
    • Used to guide Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA).
  • Thyroid scintigraphy:
    • ‘Cold’ nodules (non-functioning) require malignancy evaluation.
    • ‘Hot’ nodules (autonomous function) rarely malignant but still 10-15% risk.
  • Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA): Gold standard for cytology.
  • CT scan: Used for retrosternal goitre assessment (avoid contrast due to iodine content).

Benign vs Malignant

Benign (90% of cases):

  • Multinodular goitre, follicular adenomas, Hurthle-cell adenomas, Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, cysts.
  • Benign FNA findings: Presence of colloid, uniform nuclei.

Malignant (10% of cases):

  • Papillary carcinoma (most common), follicular carcinoma, medullary carcinoma, anaplastic carcinoma, primary lymphoma.
  • Suspicious FNA findings: Enlarged follicular cells, Hurthle cells.
  • Malignant FNA findings: Nuclear variation, inclusion bodies, pleomorphism.

Types of Thyroid Cancers

Papillary Thyroid Carcinoma (PTC)

  • Most common (78%), more common in women (2.5:1).
  • Associated with RET/PTC and TRK mutations, BRAF mutation (poor prognosis).
  • Lymph node metastases present in 1/3 of cases at diagnosis.

Follicular Thyroid Carcinoma (FTC)

  • 10% of thyroid cancers, linked to iodine deficiency.
  • Haematogenous spread → lung and bone metastases common.
  • Cannot be distinguished from follicular adenoma on FNA → requires histological capsular invasion assessment.

Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)

  • Cancer of C-cells, secretes calcitonin and carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA).
  • 25% cases hereditary (MEN-2 syndrome, RET proto-oncogene mutations).
  • Symptoms: Flushing, diarrhoea due to excess calcitonin secretion.

Anaplastic Thyroid Carcinoma

  • Highly aggressive, poor prognosis (<10% survival at 1 year).
  • Presents as rapidly enlarging mass with compressive symptoms.
  • No effective chemotherapy; treatment mainly palliative surgery + radiotherapy.

Primary Thyroid Lymphoma

  • Associated with Hashimoto’s thyroiditis.
  • Diagnosed via lymphoid cell infiltration on biopsy.
  • Treated with external beam radiotherapy.

Treatments

  • Surgery
    • Total thyroidectomy for malignant disease.
    • Indications: Obstruction, malignancy, growing nodule, patient preference.
  • Radioactive Iodine (I-131)
    • Used post-thyroidectomy for differentiated thyroid cancers (PTC, FTC).
    • Suppresses TSH to prevent recurrence.
  • TSH Suppression Therapy
    • Long-term levothyroxine to suppress TSH and reduce tumour recurrence risk.
  • Medullary Thyroid Cancer
    • No role for radioactive iodine (as C-cells do not take up iodine).
    • Surgical removal + genetic screening (RET mutations, MEN-2 screening).
  • Anaplastic Carcinoma
    • Limited treatment options: Surgery, external beam radiotherapy, tracheostomy for airway obstruction.

Summary

  • Thyroid nodules are common, but most are benign.
  • FNA is the gold standard for assessing malignancy.
  • Papillary carcinoma is the most common thyroid cancer, with an excellent prognosis.
  • Follicular carcinoma spreads via the bloodstream, requiring whole tumour evaluation.
  • Medullary carcinoma is linked to MEN-2 and requires genetic screening.
  • Anaplastic carcinoma has the worst prognosis and limited treatment options.
